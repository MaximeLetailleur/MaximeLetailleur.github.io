Final Project: What Are the Economic and Social Effects of Football Events?

Introduction

Football is the most popular sport in the world. According to FIFA, it is followed by over 5 billion fans on all continents. Moreover, Deloitte estimates the football market in 2024 at over 35 billion Euros in Europe alone. Through this economic weight and social reach, the occurrence of football events may have important economic and social effects. The objective of this project is to explore potential correlations between football events and economic and social metrics.

Data Sources & Automation

The data used in this project comes from a variety of sources. Chart 1 (cloropleth map) used a Wikipedia source directly scraped from the website, along with data from an Ipsos polling and a geographical coordinates repository. For Chart 2 I used data from FIFA's income statements. Part of those statements could be scraped directly from the FIFA website, while others only existed in PDF form. Some PDFs didn't require authorisation for scraping, so I used Tabula on Python for extraction. Chart 3 data was scraped through the IMF API, using the dataset for real GDP growth rates. Each series was batch downloaded using a loop function automatically converting the series into pandas dataframes. For Chart 4 I used the site Investing.com UK, which did not let me scrape directly (403 error). I had to download the Excel datasets manually. Chart 5 also used financial data, but to automatise the process I used the Yahoo Finance python module. For Chart 6, no precise data was available on the World Cup so I studied Premier League games instead. I used the Police UK API and wrote another loop function, inputing geographical coordinates. The loop also converted each series into a dataframe.

Challenges in Data Cleaning and Analysis

In chart 2, I encountered data cleaning challenges with the data extracted from the PDF documents, as different columns were often merged together, column names were inconsistent and revenues were expressed in different currencies. I also had issues when constructing staggered event studies (charts 3 & 4). As t0 corresponded to different dates in time, I had to manually calibrate it for each series (ex: in chart 3, France won the WC in 2018 so t0=2018, while for Spain t0=2010). Attributing a relative position to each date (t-3, t+5, etc.) required a special function on Python. For chart 4, I extracted data for t0 +/- 150 days, but had to restrict it to 30 days to make the chart more readable. For chart 6, I had to manually define the areas of interest by fetching the geo coordinates of Premier League stadiums. Building the loop with the integrated df conversion was also tricky as I wanted a separate df for each data series.

4- Conclusion

This research provides some elements to estimate the economic and social aspects of football events. The seasonal nature of the revenues perceived by the FIFA, as well as the increase in GDP growth experienced by winning nations on the year of the competition support Mello's findings of an economic boost generated by a World Cup win. However, those economic benefits do not seem to extend to the stock markets, which seems to be relatively unaffected by football competitions, even when firms are directly involved with them. Finally, the occurrence of football games tends to be associated with increased police activity, at least in key English cities. While we cannot extrapolate these findings to other countries or competitions, they support the popular view that football games can be a source of disruption of the public order.

Further research could put these results into perspective by comparing them with economic and social consequences of other popular sport events, such as the Olympic games or the cricket or rugby World Cups. Other types of social consequences could also be explored, such as the impact on domestic violence or alcohol consumption.
